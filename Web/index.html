Macro-Economic Foundations of Platform Labor

The structural shift toward gig work in India — a response to broader economic shifts, not just technological advancement.

📈

Workforce Explosion

The gig workforce expanded by 55% from 77 lakh in FY21 to 1.2 crore in FY25, now accounting for over 2% of India's total workforce. Growth is fueled by a younger urban demographic with youth unemployment at approximately 23%.

🏙️

Gap-Fillers

Platforms like Zomato, Swiggy, and Blinkit have emerged as vital "gap-fillers," absorbing unskilled labor and providing immediate, task-based income that the formal economy has struggled to offer.

The Asset-Debt Trap

04

Code on Social Security 2025

A Regulatory Watershed — November 21, 2025 marked gig workers' formal recognition in Indian labor law for the first time.

Nov 21, 2025

CoSS Implemented

Code on Social Security formally recognized gig and platform workers, bringing them under a statutory welfare framework.

Jan 2026

90-Day Engagement Rule

Workers must be engaged with an aggregator for at least 90 days to qualify for central social security benefits. Multi-appers need 120 days.

FY 2026

Turnover Contribution Begins

Zomato projected to contribute ₹43 Cr and Swiggy ₹26 Cr (1-2% of annual turnover) to a Social Security Fund.

The Exclusion Concern

Labor advocates argue the 90-day rule excludes seasonal workers and women who take breaks for caregiving, potentially leaving 40-50% of the fleet without protection despite their contribution.

05

Labor Activism & the Winter Strikes of 2025

Despite legislative progress, the end of 2025 was marked by significant labor unrest.

1.7Lakh

Gig workers logged out on New Year's Eve to protest declining real incomes and unsafe delivery conditions.

🚫

Roll Back 10-Minute Deliveries

Ultra-fast delivery promises forced dangerous driving and increased accident risk.

🔍

Algorithmic Transparency

Protests targeted "arbitrary" ID suspensions and ₹30 penalties for declining orders.

⏱️

Waiting Compensation

Unpaid time at restaurants/customer locations significantly lowers effective hourly income.

Platform Counter-Strategy

Platforms responded by offering hyper-incentives to break the strike:

  • Zomato: Up to ₹150 per order during peak hours
  • Swiggy: Potential earnings of ₹10,000 for the 48-hour NYE period

Impact Assessment

The impact was "mixed" across cities:

  • Hyderabad: Visible decrease in rider presence
  • Mumbai: Partial disruptions in grocery services
  • Other cities: Variable effectiveness
06

Algorithmic Surveillance & the Speed Crisis

The "10-minute delivery" phenomenon became a lightning rod for criticism in 2025.

⏱️

10-Minute Timer Removal

After 17,000+ traffic violations by delivery riders in a single week, the Labour Ministry directed quick-commerce platforms to remove 10-minute delivery time limits and branding from their apps.

Zomato CEO defended the practice, stating speed was due to dark store proximity, not rider speed — but the government mandate prevailed.

🤖

Algorithmic Accountability

The Economic Survey 2025-26 highlighted concerns about "algorithmic bias" and burnout, calling for "minimum per-task earnings" that include waiting time.

🔴

40% of gig workers earn below ₹15,000/month — challenging the narrative of high-earning gig "partners".

Fiscal & Taxation Realities

New indirect taxes and complex direct tax reporting further complicate the gig worker financial ecosystem.

📋

A landmark GST Council ruling imposed 18% GST on delivery fees.

Income Tax Compliance

Workers report as "Profits and Gains of Business or Profession":

ITR-3

Claim actual expenses (fuel, mobile, maintenance) as deductions.

ITR-4 (Sugam)

Presumptive taxation under Section 44ADA — simplified but limits expense claims.

Platform earnings now tracked via Form 26AS.

Supply-Side Stress: Restaurant Perspective

Growing friction between platforms and restaurant partners threatens the model's sustainability.

Where Does the Order Value Go?

Commission 25%
Ads ~15%
Discounts ~15%

Restaurant ~35%

After ads, discounts, and fees, restaurants may receive as low as 35% of total order value.

🎯 Tryout Ads Issue

Originally a 15% revenue-sharing model for visibility, some owners reported these morphed into 33% deductions without clear consent.

🔄 Direct Ordering Movement

Restaurants increasingly encourage customers to order via their own websites or phone calls to bypass the 30% price markup on aggregator apps.

Strategic Outlook

The Indian gig economy in 2026 is defined by a "new normal" of regulation and resistance. The CoSS 2025 provides a baseline of dignity and safety, yet implementation remains a battlefield between corporate profitability, government fiscal needs, and worker survival.

For the duopoly of Zomato and Swiggy, the coming years will be less about market share and more about managing the social contract with the 1.2 crore individuals who power their growth. The success of this model hinges on whether the "delivery partner" can truly transition from a disposable unit of labor to a protected and productive stakeholder in India's digital future.
